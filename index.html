04 Record timeline
~300 BCE
Euclid proves primes are infinite
In the Elements, Euclid shows that no finite list can contain all primes. The proof - two lines, still taught today - is one of the oldest logical arguments in mathematics.
theory
~200 BCE
Eratosthenes' Sieve
The first systematic algorithm for finding primes. Eratosthenes described a method for sieving out composites that remains conceptually unchanged 2200 years later.
algorithm
1202
Fibonacci introduces his sequence
Leonardo of Pisa's Liber Abaci introduces the sequence 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13… Fibonacci primes - Fibonacci numbers that are also prime - remain an active research area today.
theory
1536
Largest known prime: 524,287
Hudalrichus Regius verifies that 524,287 = 2¹⁹ − 1 is prime. The first recorded large prime found by direct verification, not just conjecture.
2¹⁹ − 1 = 524,287
record
1640
Fermat's Little Theorem
Fermat states in a letter that aᵖ ≡ a (mod p) for any prime p. He offers no proof. The theorem would later become the backbone of primality testing and public-key cryptography.
theory
1644
Mersenne publishes his list
Marin Mersenne claims primes of the form 2ᵖ − 1 for p = 2, 3, 5, 7, 13, 17, 19, 31, 67, 127, 257. He got five wrong - but the Mersenne prime hunt he started never stopped.
theory
1772
Euler finds the 8th Mersenne prime
Leonhard Euler verifies by hand that 2³¹ − 1 = 2,147,483,647 is prime. He is 65 and nearly blind. The calculation takes months and remains one of the most impressive feats of mental arithmetic in history.
2³¹ − 1 = 2,147,483,647
record
1859
Riemann's 8-page paper
Bernhard Riemann submits "On the Number of Primes Less Than a Given Magnitude." In 8 pages he connects prime distribution to the zeros of a complex function and states a hypothesis still unproven 165 years later.
theory
1896
Prime Number Theorem proved
Hadamard and de la Vallée Poussin independently prove that π(x) ~ x/ln(x). Conjectured by Gauss at 15, proved 90 years later. One of the great achievements of 19th-century mathematics.
theory
1952
First computer-found prime
Raphael Robinson uses the SWAC computer to find five Mersenne primes in a single evening - more than had been found in the previous century combined. The era of computational prime hunting begins.
2²⁵²¹ − 1 (largest of the five)
record
1976
Miller-Rabin primality test
Gary Miller and Michael Rabin develop a fast probabilistic test for primality. It runs in milliseconds and underlies the generation of cryptographic keys. Every RSA prime today is tested this way.
algorithm
1977
RSA encryption invented
Rivest, Shamir and Adleman publish their public-key cryptosystem. Security rests entirely on the hardness of factoring large primes. Every HTTPS connection in the world now depends on prime numbers.
algorithm
1996
GIMPS founded
George Woltman launches the Great Internet Mersenne Prime Search. For the first time, thousands of volunteers worldwide pool CPU cycles toward a single mathematical goal. It will find the 13 largest known primes in history.
record
2002
AKS: primes in polynomial time
Agrawal, Kayal and Saxena prove that primality can be determined deterministically in polynomial time. A theoretical landmark - though Miller-Rabin remains faster in practice.
algorithm
2013
Zhang's bounded prime gaps
Yitang Zhang, an unknown mathematician working at a Subway sandwich shop, proves that infinitely many prime pairs differ by at most 70 million. The first finite bound ever proven. Within a year, collaborative work reduced it to 246.
theory
2024
Largest known prime: 41+ million digits
Luke Durant, a former NVIDIA engineer, uses a network of GPUs across 17 countries to find M136279841. It has 41,024,320 digits. If printed, it would fill roughly 25,000 pages. Verified by GIMPS in October 2024.
2¹³⁶²⁷⁹⁸⁴¹ − 1
